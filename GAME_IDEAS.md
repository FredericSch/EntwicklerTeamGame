# Future Minigame Concepts

This document outlines ideas for future minigames and expansions, focusing on playful concepts that teach software engineering skills without requiring prior IT knowledge.

## Core Principles for New Games
- **Playful Interaction:** The game should feel like a creative puzzle or a fun challenge, not a test.
- **No IT Jargon:** Concepts should be taught through gameplay mechanics, not technical terms.
- **Real-World Connection:** The skill learned should clearly connect to how real software is made.
- **Mobile-First:** Interactions must be simple, visual, and work well on a phone.

---

## Tier 1: High-Priority Game Ideas

These ideas are the best fit for the next development phase. They are highly visual, interactive, and teach valuable, non-obvious software skills.

### 1. Creative Connections
- **Concept:** APIs and Service Integration
- **Learning Goal:** Understand how different apps and services can "talk" to each other to create new features.
- **Gameplay:** Players see two app mockups (e.g., a music app and a social media app). They are given a set of "connector" blocks with simple icons (like "Song Title," "Album Art," "Friend List"). The goal is to drag the right blocks into place to create a new, combined feature, like "Share what you're listening to in a post."
- **Why it works:** It visualizes the abstract concept of an API as a simple, creative matching puzzle.

### 2. The Filter Factory
- **Concept:** Algorithms and Logical Filtering
- **Learning Goal:** Learn how software uses rules to sort, filter, and process information.
- **Gameplay:** A stream of items (e.g., social media posts with different icons, colors, and tags) flows on a conveyor belt. Players must place a series of "filter gates" (e.g., "Only posts with a picture," "Not posts from 'BotUser'," "Must be blue") in the correct order to only let specific items reach the end.
- **Why it works:** It turns the logic of an algorithm or a database query into a fun, hands-on machine-building game.

### 3. Empathy Map
- **Concept:** User-Centered Design and Accessibility
- **Learning Goal:** Think about the needs of different users, including those with disabilities.
- **Gameplay:** Players are shown a simple app screen (e.g., a video player). They are then given a "user persona" card, such as "Someone with low vision" or "Someone on a slow, unstable internet connection." They must then tap on parts of the UI that would be frustrating for that user and choose the best fix (e.g., for low vision, tap the small text and choose "Increase font size and contrast").
- **Why it works:** It directly teaches empathy, a core skill in UX design, by putting the player in someone else's shoes.

## Tier 2: Promising Future Concepts

These ideas are also strong but may require more design work to make them perfectly intuitive.

### 4. The Time Thief
- **Concept:** Performance and Optimization
- **Learning Goal:** Understand that some code or features are "heavier" than others and can slow an app down.
- **Gameplay:** Players see a simple progress bar representing an app's loading time. Below it are several "feature" blocks (e.g., "High-Res Video," "Complex Animation," "Basic Text"). Each block has a "weight" and a "value" (represented by stars). The goal is to choose a combination of features that provides the most value without exceeding the time limit.
- **Why it works:** It simplifies the complex topic of performance optimization into a simple, visual budget-balancing puzzle.

### 5. The Social Engineer
- **Concept:** Cybersecurity and Critical Thinking (Defense)
- **Learning Goal:** Learn to spot phishing, suspicious links, and manipulative "dark patterns" in fake emails and social media posts.
- **Gameplay:** A more advanced version of the White-Hat Mission. Players are presented with a series of simulated emails, login screens, and social media feeds. They must identify the "trick" by tapping on the suspicious element (e.g., a misspelled URL in a link, a fake "low stock" warning, a password field that isn't masked).
- **Why it works:** It's a direct and practical game of "spot the trap," teaching digital literacy and defensive thinking in a safe environment.

## Future Development Roadmap

1.  **Implement Tier 1:** Focus on developing "Creative Connections," "The Filter Factory," and "Empathy Map." These three games cover a wide range of software engineering skills (backend, logic, frontend/UX) and are the most innovative additions.
2.  **Gather Feedback:** After releasing the Tier 1 games, collect feedback from students and teachers on which concepts were the most fun and effective.
3.  **Refine and Develop Tier 2:** Use the feedback to refine the designs for "The Time Thief" and "The Social Engineer" before beginning development.
4.  **Expand Existing Games:** Consider adding new level packs or challenges to the most popular existing games (like Bug Hunt and Design-Duell) based on user requests.
